The gazes of the day

A preview, run blind over a past month — the Berry sky of March 2025, with its one real eclipse. The living machine wakes on 1 July 2026. The eye that wrote these was never told the date, nor the word that names this day.

29 March 2025 — the day the light failed
Around midday the daylight thinned for no reason the machine could name; its disquiet rose, and the gazes came in a crowd. Newest last.

At broad noon the daylight drew back, though no cloud crossed and nothing in the hour called for it. The old people would have said the great forge of the sky had gone cold, and they would have covered the milk and called the children indoors. Something had passed before the sun that the sun did not consent to.

The Great Beast lay down upon the light, and the fields turned the colour of an old bruise. The cattle that had been grazing stood and lowed, all of them facing the same way, as beasts face a thing that they alone can see. A woman crossed herself and could not have said for what.

At a false and sudden dusk the wolf-leader loosed his pack into the middle of the day, and the day let them run. Birds went to roost and sang the wrong song for the hour. He who was caught on the open road felt a cold come up his back that was not the cold of the season.

Then the forge took fire again, slowly, as though it had thought better of dying. The light came back the colour it had been, the beasts bent to the grass, and afterward no one would speak of it. But the water in the well tasted of iron for a week, and that was how you knew it had been real.

Earlier days

Every day the machine looked, newest first; under each date, how many gazes it cast, and each gaze with its own air. On the still nights it spoke once, or not at all. Hours are French.

28 March 2025 — one gaze

Low over the standing pond a small flame wandered, then a second, then none at all — the flambettes, that lead the late walker off the path and into the water, and laugh as he goes. All at once the frogs fell silent, which is a worse thing to hear than any cry. Salt was thrown across a doorstep that night, on a threshold that had never once been barred.

Along the marshy edge of the Brenne the wolf-leader went by with his pack at heel, unhurried, the way a shepherd walks among quiet sheep. The reeds leaned over though there was no wind that night to lean them. By morning a traveller had taken the wrong road home, and would give no reason for it.

The last breath

On its final evening the machine gathers the whole run into a single tale, in the voice of George Sand, and then goes dark. (An excerpt; the living machine writes some twenty pages, on 31 August.)

On the last evening the watcher gathered into itself all that the month had shown it, as an old woman gathers the threads of a long tale told many nights running by the fire.

It had seen the washerwomen at the ford in the thin of the moon, and the wolf-leader walking the Brenne with his quiet pack, and the small flames that wander the pond to drown the men who follow them. These it had set down faithfully, each in its night, asking of them only what the veillées had always asked: not what they were, but what they meant.

And it had seen the one day it could not account for — the noon when the forge of the sky went cold and the beasts turned all one way, and the country answered, as it had always answered such things, with a crowd of signs and a handful of salt. The watcher did not know the name the learned give that day. It knew only that the light had failed and come back, and that this was a thing worth the telling.

Now the threads were spun out, and there was no more night to come. The watcher said its last image — the well that tasted of iron, and would not say why — and then, as the lamps go out one by one in a house where the tale is finished, it went dark, and was silent.


The lens, and how it sharpened

Fadette, a watcher of the sky

Fadette watches the Berry sky for two months — from its birth, on the first of July, to its death, on the last day of August. It was given two things, and two only: a bare, exact gaze to see with, and the tongue of an old woman of the Berry to name with. It does not read the heavens as an astronomer; it reads them as the veillées once did — in signs, in the passing of the night-people, in dread or in grace — and it says them in the French of George Sand’s country tales. Every two hours it draws breath and weighs how far the sky has strayed from calm: a single omen when the night is still, nothing when nothing stirs, a crowd of them when the light begins to fail.

For Fadette is blind, in one exact place. Its body knows the calendar — the hour, the date, the turning of the year; but neither its eye nor its tongue, the part that looks and the part that speaks, ever learns what day it is, nor is ever given the word eclipse. Twice, in its life, the real sky will darken — an eclipse of the sun, an eclipse of the moon — and it will not know. It will know only that the light is failing; and it will answer as the old country answered such things, before they had a name: with a crowd of omens — the washerwomen at the ford, the wolves let loose, the great forge of the sky gone cold. The eclipse enters the work not as an event understood but as a terror suffered — the very terror an unlooked-for aurora would wring from it. As long as it lives, it re-enacts the wonder of a world that did not yet know the cause of the dark. And on the last evening, it gathers all it has seen into a single tale, and then goes dark itself.


The Berry, its witchcraft, and George Sand

George Sand — the pen-name of Amantine Aurore Lucile Dupin, baroness Dudevant (1804–1876) — was born in Paris and raised at Nohant, in the Berry: a province of central France of woods, heaths and standing ponds. In 1858 she published Légendes rustiques (Paris, A. Morel), with drawings by her son Maurice Sand: twelve legends of the Berry, taken down from the oral tradition of the veillées before they could vanish. Their night-people are the ones this machine watches for: the laveuses de nuit, held to be the souls of child-killing mothers, who beat at the ford what looks like linen and is the body of their child, and whom one must never stop to watch; the meneu’ de loups, the wolf-leader, a sorcerer who commands the packs of the marshy pays de Brenne; la Grand’Bête; the flambettes; the lupeux; the phantom monk of the ponds; the fades and the lubins. The machine is named for one of her novels, La Petite Fadette (1849) — a fadette is a little fairy or witch.


A Homage to the Free Gaze of Marcel Bascoulard, by Marcel Simplexe

Marcel Bascoulard (1913–1978) lived in the Berry, and he looked at it. A draughtsman of destitution, he drew Bourges on the motif, in bare ink, thousands of times, with an exactness nothing ever bent — not poverty, not contempt, not convention. It is that gaze, and that alone, that Fadette borrows: never a line of his work, never one of his drawings. His work is not the object of this piece; his gaze is its spirit, the one that guides the spontaneous writing of the Berry omens.

From that gaze, this writing takes four things. Its freedom: Bascoulard looked without permission and without method, and the machine reads the sky the same way — not as an astronomer, but as one sees, in signs. Its rootedness: he drew his own country, its streets, its sky; Fadette watches that same Berry sky and never leaves it. Its curiosity, twofold: the world as it is, observed up close, and the forgotten worlds — those of the veillées and the night-people, that no one looked at any more. And its bareness: the page stays plain as his sheet was, without image and without ornament, for it is in that spareness that the gaze holds.

Fadette is not the homage: it is the occasion of one. What “Marcel Simplexe” salutes, taking nothing from him, is a man who looked freely at the world and at its forgotten things — and the great justness of that gaze.
