The last legend

On the thirty-first, the machine draws its last breath and composes a tale from all it has written across two months — by a sovereign model, upon the season’s fragments. Then it falls silent. (Opening, specimen.)

They kept the fire late that summer, and the talk came round, as it always came round, to the ford and to what beats there when no one washes. The old ones had the names ready — the washerwomen, the meneu’ de loups, the fire that walks — and the young laughed, as the young always laugh, a little too quickly, one ear turned toward the black window.

Twice that season the day went wrong. Once toward the middle of the warm months, once at their end, the light had thinned at the ill hour, and the country remembered, all at once and unbidden, every fear it pretended to have forgotten. The cattle had known first. They always know first.

What follows is not an explanation, for the country never wanted one. It is only the account of a night-watcher who looked when she was told not to look, and saw the linen rise, and lived — and could never afterward say the word for what she had seen, save that it was white, and patient, and in no haste at all.

[ the tale runs to some twenty pages ]



The Berry, its witchery, and George Sand

George Sand — pen name of Amantine Aurore Lucile Dupin, Baroness Dudevant (1804–1876) — was born in Paris and raised at Nohant, in the Berry: a province of central France, of woods, heath, and sleeping ponds. In 1858 she published the Légendes rustiques (Paris, A. Morel), with drawings by her son Maurice Sand: twelve legends of the Berry, gathered from the oral tradition of the night-watches. Their people of the night are the ones this machine watches for: the laveuses de nuit, the night-washerwomen, held to be the souls of mothers who killed their children, beating at the ford what seems to be linen and is the body of their child; the meneu’ de loups, the sorcerer who commands the packs of the Brenne country; the Grand’Bête; the flambettes; the lupeux; the ghost monk of the ponds; the fades and the lubins. The machine bears the name of one of her novels, La Petite Fadette (1849).
